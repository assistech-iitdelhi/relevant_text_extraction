41: 3.1.2 Law of constant proportions

42: Lavoisier, along with other scientists, noted that many compounds were composed of two or more elements and each such compound had the same elements in the same proportions, irrespective of where the compound came from or who prepared it.

43: In a compound such as water, the ratio of the mass of hydrogen to the mass of oxygen is always 1:8, whatever the source of water

44: Thus, if 9 g of water is decomposed, 1 g of hydrogen and 8 g of oxygen are always obtained

45: Similarly in ammonia, nitrogen and hydrogen are always present in the ratio 14:3 by mass, whatever the method or the source from which it is obtained.

46: This led to the law of constant proportions which is also known as the law of definite proportions

47: This law was stated by Proust as In a chemical substance the elements are always present in definite proportions by mass .

48: The next problem faced by scientists was to give appropriate explanations of these laws

49: British chemist John Dalton provided the basic theory about the nature of matter

50: Dalton picked up the idea of divisibility of matter, which was till then just a philosophy

51: He took the name atoms as given by the Greeks and said that the smallest particles of matter are atoms

52: His theory was based on the laws of chemical combination

53: Dalton s atomic theory provided an explanation for the law of conservation of mass and the law of definite proportions.

61: According to Dalton s atomic theory, all matter, whether an element, a compound or a mixture is composed of small particles called atoms

62: The postulates of this theory may be stated as follows:

63: (i) All matter is made of very tiny particles called atoms, which participate in chemical reactions.

64: (ii) Atoms are indivisible particles, which cannot be created or destroyed in a chemical reaction.

65: (iii) Atoms of a given element are identical in mass and chemical properties.

66: (iv) Atoms of different elements have different masses and chemical properties.

67: (v) Atoms combine in the ratio of small whole numbers to form compounds.

68: (vi) The relative number and kinds of atoms are constant in a given compound.

291: 3.5.3 Mole concept

292: Take an example of the reaction of hydrogen and oxygen to form water:

293: 2H2+ O2 2H2O.

294: The above reaction indicates that

295: (i) two molecules of hydrogen combine with one molecule of oxygen to form two molecules of water, or

296: (ii) 4 u of hydrogen molecules combine with 32 u of oxygen molecules to form 36 u of water molecules.

297: We can infer from the above equation that the quantity of a substance can be characterised by its mass or the number of molecules

298: But, a chemical reaction equation indicates directly the number of atoms or molecules taking part in the reaction

299: Therefore, it is more convenient to refer to the quantity of a substance in terms of the number of its molecules or atoms, rather than their masses

300: So, a new unit mole was introduced

301: The mole, symbol mol, is the SI unit of amount of substance

302: One mole contains exactly 6.02214076 1023 elementary entities

303: This number is the fixed numerical value of the Avogadro constant, NA, when expressed in the unit mol 1 and is called the Avogadro number*.

304: Figure 3.5: Relationship between mole, Avogadro number and mass

305: The amount of substance, symbol n, of a system is a measure of the number of specified elementary entities

306: An elementary entity may be an atom, a molecule, an ion, an electron, any other particle or specified group of particles

307: The mole is the amount of substance of a system that contains 6.02214076 1023 specified elementary entities.

308: 1 mole (of anything) = 6.022 1023 in number,

309: as, 1 dozen = 12 nos.

310: Besides being related to a number, a mole has one more advantage over a dozen or a gross

311: This advantage is that mass of 1 mole of a particular substance is also fixed.

312: The mass of 1 mole of a substance is equal to its relative atomic or molecular mass in grams

313: The atomic mass of an element gives us the mass of one atom of that element in atomic mass units (u)

314: To get the mass of 1 mole of atom of that element, that is, molar mass, we have to take the same numerical value but change the units from u to g

315: Molar mass of atoms is also known as gram atomic mass

316: For example, atomic mass of hydrogen=1u

317: So, gram atomic mass of hydrogen = 1 g.

318: 1 u hydrogen has only 1 atom of hydrogen 1 g hydrogen has 1 mole atoms, that is,6.022 1023 atoms of hydrogen.

319: Similarly,

320: 16 u oxygen has only 1 atom of oxygen, 16 g oxygen has 1 mole atoms, that is, 6.022 1023 atoms of oxygen.

321: To find the gram molecular mass or molar mass of a molecule, we keep the numerical value the same as the molecular mass, but simply change units as above from u to g

322: For example, as we have already calculated, molecular mass of water (H2O) is 18 u

323: From here we understand that

324: 18 u water has only 1 molecule of water,

325: 18 g water has 1 mole molecules of water, that is, 6.022 1023 molecules of water.
