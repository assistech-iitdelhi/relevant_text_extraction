3: From the last chapter, we recall that all living organisms are made of cells

4: In unicellular organisms, a single cell performs all basic functions

5: For example, in Amoeba, a single cell carries out movement, intake of food, gaseous exchange and excretion

6: But in multicellular organisms there are millions of cells

7: Most of these cells are specialised to carry out specific functions

8: Each specialised function is taken up by a different group of cells

9: Since these cells carry out only a particular function, they do it very efficiently

10: In human beings, muscle cells contract and relax to cause movement, nerve cells carry messages, blood flows to transport oxygen, food, hormones and waste material and so on

11: In plants, vascular tissues conduct food and water from one part of the plant to other parts

12: So, multi-cellular organisms show division of labour

13: Cells specialising in one function are often grouped together in the body

14: This means that a particular function is carried out by a cluster of cells at a definite place in the body

15: This cluster of cells, called a tissue, is arranged and designed so as to give the highest possible efficiency of function

16: Blood, phloem and muscle are all examples of tissues.

17: A group of cells that are similar in structure and/or work together to achieve a particular function forms a tissue.

18: 6.1 Are Plants and Animals Made of Same Types of Tissues

19: Let us compare their structure and functions

20: Do plants and animals have the same structure

21: Do they both perform similar functions

22: There are noticeable differences between the two

23: Plants are stationary or fixed they don t move

24: Since they have to be upright, they have a large quantity of supportive tissue

25: The supportive tissue generally has dead cells.

26: Animals on the other hand move around in search of food, mates and shelter

27: They consume more energy as compared to plants

28: Most of the tissues they contain are living.

29: Another difference between animals and plants is in the pattern of growth

30: The growth in plants is limited to certain regions, while this is not so in animals

31: There are some tissues in plants that divide throughout their life

32: These tissues are localised in certain regions

33: Based on the dividing capacity of the tissues, various plant tissues can be classified as growing or meristematic tissue and permanent tissue

34: Cell growth in animals is more uniform

35: So, there is no such demarcation of dividing and nondividing regions in animals.

36: The structural organisation of organs and organ systems is far more specialised and localised in complex animals than even in very complex plants

37: This fundamental difference reflects the different modes of life pursued by these two major groups of organisms, particularly in their different feeding methods Also, they are differently adapted for a sedentary existence on one hand (plants) and active locomotion on the other (animals), contributing to this difference in organ system design.

195: 6.3.1 Epithelial tissue

196: The covering or protective tissues in the animal body are epithelial tissues

197: Epithelium covers most organs and cavities within the body

198: It also forms a barrier to keep different body systems separate

199: The skin, the lining of the mouth, the lining of blood vessels, lung alveoli and kidney tubules are all made of epithelial tissue

200: Epithelial tissue cells are tightly packed and form a continuous sheet

201: They have only a small amount of cementing material between them and almost no intercellular spaces

202: Obviously, anything entering or leaving the body must cross at least one layer of epithelium

203: As a result, the permeability of the cells of various epithelia play an important role in regulating the exchange of materials between the body and the external environment and also between different parts of the body

204: Regardless of the type, all epithelium is usually separated from the underlying tissue by an extracellular fibrous basement membrane.

205: Different epithelia (Figure 6.9) show differing structures that correlate with their unique functions

206: For example, in cells lining blood vessels or lung alveoli, where transportation of substances occurs through a selectively permeable surface, there is a simple flat kind of epithelium

207: This is called the simple squamous epithelium (squama means scale of skin)

208: Simple squamous epithelial cells are extremely thin and flat and form a delicate lining

209: The oesophagus and the lining of the mouth are also covered with squamous epithelium

210: The skin, which protects the body, is also made of squamous epithelium

211: Skin epithelial cells are arranged in many layers to prevent wear and tear

212: Since they are arranged in a pattern of layers, the epithelium is called stratified squamous epithelium.

213: Figure 6.9: Different types of epithelial tissues

214: Where absorption and secretion occur, as in the inner lining of the intestine, tall epithelial cells are present

215: This columnar (meaning pillar-like ) epithelium facilitates movement across the epithelial barrier

216: In the respiratory tract, the columnar epithelial tissue also has cilia, which are hair-like projections on the outer surfaces of epithelial cells

217: These cilia can move, and their movement pushes the mucus forward to clear it

218: This type of epithelium is thus ciliated columnar epithelium.

219: Cuboidal epithelium (with cube-shaped cells) forms the lining of kidney tubules and ducts of salivary glands, where it provides mechanical support

220: Epithelial cells often acquire additional specialisation as gland cells, which can secrete substances at the epithelial surface

221: Sometimes a portion of the epithelial tissue folds inward, and a multicellular gland is formed

222: This is glandular epithelium.
