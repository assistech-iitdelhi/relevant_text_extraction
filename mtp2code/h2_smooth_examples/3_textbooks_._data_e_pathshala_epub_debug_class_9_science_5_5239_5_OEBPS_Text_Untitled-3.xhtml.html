95: 5.2.1 Plasma membrane or cell membrane

96: This is the outermost covering of the cell that separates the contents of the cell from its external environment

97: The plasma membrane allows or permits the entry and exit of some materials in and out of the cell

98: It also prevents movement of some other materials

99: The cell membrane, therefore, is called a selectively permeable membrane.

100: How does the movement of substances take place into the cell

101: How do substances move out of the cell

102: Some substances like carbon dioxide or oxygen can move across the cell membrane by a process called diffusion

103: We have studied the process of diffusion in earlier chapters

104: We saw that there is spontaneous movement of a substance from a region of high concentration to a region where its concentration is low.

105: Something similar to this happens in cells when, for example, some substance like CO2 (which is cellular waste and requires to be excreted out by the cell) accumulates in high concentrations inside the cell

106: In the cell s external environment, the concentration of CO2 is low as compared to that inside the cell

107: As soon as there is a difference of concentration of CO2 inside and outside a cell, CO2 moves out of the cell, from a region of high concentration, to a region of low concentration outside the cell by the process of diffusion

108: Similarly, O2 enters the cell by the process of diffusion when the level or concentration of O2 inside the cell decreases

109: Thus, diffusion plays an important role in gaseous exchange between the cells as well as the cell and its external environment.

110: Water also obeys the law of diffusion

111: The movement of water molecules through such a selectively permeable membrane is called osmosis

112: The movement of water across the plasma membrane is also affected by the amount of substance dissolved in water

113: Thus, osmosis is the passage of water from a region of high water concentration through a selectively permeable membrane to a region of low water concentration till equilibrium is reached.

114: What will happen if we put an animal cell or a plant cell into a solution of sugar or salt in water

115: One of the following three things could happen:

116: If the medium surrounding the cell has a higher water concentration than the cell, meaning that the outside solution is very dilute, the cell will gain water by osmosis

117: Such a solution is known as a hypotonic solution.

118: Water molecules are free to pass across the cell membrane in both directions, but more water will come into the cell than will leave

119: The net (overall) result is that water enters the cell

120: The cell is likely to swell up.

121: If the medium has exactly the same water concentration as the cell, there will be no net movement of water across the cell membrane

122: Such a solution is known as an isotonic solution.

123: Water crosses the cell membrane in both directions, but the amount going in is the same as the amount going out, so there is no overall movement of water

124: The cell will stay the same size.

125: If the medium has a lower concentration of water than the cell, meaning that it is a very concentrated solution, the cell will lose water by osmosis

126: Such a solution is known as a hypertonic solution.

127: Again, water crosses the cell membrane in both directions, but this time more water leaves the cell than enters it

128: Therefore the cell will shrink.

129: Thus, osmosis is a special case of diffusion through a selectively permeable membrane

253: 5.2.5 Cell organelles

254: Every cell has a membrane around it to keep its own contents separate from the external environment

255: Large and complex cells, including cells from multicellular organisms, need a lot of chemical activities to support their complicated structure and function

256: To keep these activities of different kinds separate from each other, these cells use membrane-bound little structures (or organelles ) within themselves

257: This is one of the features of the eukaryotic cells that distinguish them from prokaryotic cells

258: Some of these organelles are visible only with an electron microscope.

259: We have talked about the nucleus in a previous section

260: Some important examples of cell organelles which we will discuss now are: endoplasmic reticulum, Golgi apparatus, lysosomes, mitochondria and plastids

261: They are important because they carry out some very crucial functions in cells.

262: 5.2.5 (i) Endoplasmic reticulum (ER)

263: The endoplasmic reticulum (ER) is a large network of membrane-bound tubes and sheets

264: It looks like long tubules or round or oblong bags (vesicles)

265: The ER membrane is similar in structure to the plasma membrane

266: There are two types of ER rough endoplasmic reticulum (RER) and smooth endoplasmic reticulum (SER)

267: RER looks rough under a microscope because it has particles called ribosomes attached to its surface

268: The ribosomes, which are present in all active cells, are the sites of protein manufacture

269: The manufactured proteins are then sent to various places in the cell depending on need, using the ER

270: The SER helps in the manufacture of fat molecules, or lipids, important for cell function

271: Some of these proteins and lipids help in building the cell membrane

272: This process is known as membrane biogenesis

273: Some other proteins and lipids function as enzymes and hormones

274: Although the ER varies greatly in appearance in different cells, it always forms a network system.

275: Figure 5.5: Animal cell

276: Thus, one function of the ER is to serve as channels for the transport of materials (especially proteins) between various regions of the cytoplasm or between the cytoplasm and the nucleus

277: The ER also functions as a cytoplasmic framework providing a surface for some of the biochemical activities of the cell

278: In the liver cells of the group of animals called vertebrates (see Chapter 7), SER plays a crucial role in detoxifying many poisons and drugs.

279: Figure 5.6: Plant cell

280: 5.2.5 (ii) Golgi apparatus

281: The Golgi apparatus, first described by Camillo Golgi, consists of a system of membrane-bound vesicles (flattened sacs) arranged approximately parallel to each other in stacks called cisterns

282: These membranes often have connections with the membranes of ER and therefore constitute another portion of a complex cellular membrane system.

283: The material synthesised near the ER is packaged and dispatched to various targets inside and outside the cell through the Golgi apparatus

284: Its functions include the storage, modification and packaging of products in vesicles

285: In some cases, complex sugars may be made from simple sugars in the Golgi apparatus

286: The Golgi apparatus is also involved in the formation of lysosomes [see 5.2.5 (iii)].

287: Camillo Golgi was born at Corteno near Brescia in 1843

288: He studied medicine at the University of Pavia

289: After graduating in 1865, he continued to work in Pavia at the Hospital of St

290: Matteo

291: At that time most of his investigations were concerned with the nervous system, In 1872 he accepted the post of Chief Medical Officer at the Hospital for the Chronically Sick at Abbiategrasso

292: He first started his investigations into the nervous system in a little kitchen of this hospital, which he had converted into a laboratory

293: However, the work of greatest importance, which Golgi carried out was a revolutionary method of staining individual nerve and cell structures

294: This method is referred to as the black reaction

295: This method uses a weak solution of silver nitrate and is particularly valuable in tracing the processes and most delicate ramifications of cells

296: All through his life, he continued to work on these lines, modifying and improving this technique

297: Golgi received the highest honours and awards in recognition of his work

298: He shared the Nobel prize in 1906 with Santiago Ramony Cajal for their work on the structure of the nervous system.

299: 5.2.5 (iii) Lysosomes

300: Structurally, lysosomes are membrane-bound sacs filled with digestive enzymes

301: These enzymes are made by RER

302: Lysosomes are a kind of waste disposal system of the cell

303: These help to keep the cell clean by digesting any foreign material as well as worn-out cell organelles

304: Foreign materials entering the cell, such as bacteria or food, as well as old organelles end up in the lysosomes, which break complex substances into simpler substances

305: Lysosomes are able to do this because they contain powerful digestive enzymes capable of breaking down all organic material

306: During the disturbance in cellular metabolism, for example, when the cell gets damaged, lysosomes may burst and the enzymes digest their own cell

307: Therefore, lysosomes are also known as the suicide bags of a cell.

308: 5.2.5 (iv) Mitochondria

309: Mitochondria are known as the powerhouses of the cell

310: Mitochondria have two membrane coverings

311: The outer membrane is porous while the inner membrane is deeply folded

312: These folds increase surface area for ATP-generating chemical reactions

313: The energy required for various chemical activities needed for life is released by mitochondria in the form of ATP (Adenosine triphopshate) molecules

314: ATP is known as the energy currency of the cell

315: The body uses energy stored in ATP for making new chemical compounds and for mechanical work.

316: Mitochondria are strange organelles in the sense that they have their own DNA and ribosomes

317: Therefore, mitochondria are able to make some of their own proteins.

318: 5.2.5 (v) Plastids

319: Plastids are present only in plant cells

320: There are two types of plastids chromoplasts (coloured plastids) and leucoplasts (white or colourless plastids)

321: Chromoplasts containing the pigment chlorophyll are known as chloroplasts

322: Chloroplasts are important for photosynthesis in plants

323: Chloroplasts also contain various yellow or orange pigments in addition to chlorophyll

324: Leucoplasts are primarily organelles in which materials such as starch, oils and protein granules are stored.

325: The internal organisation of the Chloroplast consists of numerous membrane layers embedded in a material called the stroma

326: These are similar to mitochondria in external structure

327: Like the mitochondria, plastids also have their own DNA and ribosomes.

328: 5.2.5 (vi) Vacuoles

329: Vacuoles are storage sacs for solid or liquid contents

330: Vacuoles are small sized in animal cells while plant cells have very large vacuoles

331: The central vacuole of some plant cells may occupy 50-90% of the cell volume.

332: In plant cells vacuoles are full of cell sap and provide turgidity and rigidity to the cell

333: Many substances of importance in the life of the plant cell are stored in vacuoles

334: These include amino acids, sugars, various organic acids and some proteins

335: In single-celled organisms like Amoeba, the food vacuole contains the food items that the Amoeba has consumed

336: In some unicellular organisms, specialised vacuoles also play important roles in expelling excess water and some wastes from the cell.
