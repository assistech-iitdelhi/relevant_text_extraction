364: 2.5 What are the Types of Pure Substances

365: On the basis of their chemical composition, substances can be classified either as elements or compounds.

366: 2.5.1 Elements

367: Robert Boyle was the first scientist to use the term element in 1661

368: Antoine Laurent Lavoisier (1743-94), a French chemist, was the first to establish an experimentally useful definition of an element

369: He defined an element as a basic form of matter that cannot be broken down into simpler substances by chemical reactions.

370: Elements can be normally divided into metals, non-metals and metalloids.

371: Metals usually show some or all of the following properties:

372: They have a lustre (shine).

373: They have silvery-grey or golden-yellow colour.

374: They conduct heat and electricity.

375: They are ductile (can be drawn into wires).

376: They are malleable (can be hammered into thin sheets).

377: They are sonorous (make a ringing sound when hit).

378: Examples of metals are gold, silver, copper, iron, sodium, potassium etc

379: Mercury is the only metal that is liquid at room temperature.

380: Non-metals usually show some or all of the following properties:

381: They display a variety of colours.

382: They are poor conductors of heat and electricity.

383: They are not lustrous, sonorous or malleable.

384: Examples of non-metals are hydrogen, oxygen, iodine, carbon (coal, coke), bromine, chlorine etc

385: Some elements have intermediate properties between those of metals and non-metals, they are called metalloids; examples are boron, silicon, germanium etc.

386: More to know

387: The number of elements known at present are more than 100

388: Ninety-two elements are naturally occurring and the rest are man-made.

389: Majority of the elements are solid.

390: Eleven elements are in gaseous state at room temperature.

391: Two elements are liquid at room temperature mercury and bromine.

392: Elements, gallium and cesium become liquid at a temperature slightly above room temperature (303 K).
