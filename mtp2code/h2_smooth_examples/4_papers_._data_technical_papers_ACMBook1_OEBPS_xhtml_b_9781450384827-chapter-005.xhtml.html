1: 5Event Mining Applications

2: Event mining algorithms and tools can be utilized in a wide range of applications in engineering (e.g., intrusion detection and network security and flow classification), business (e.g., fraud detection, decision support systems, and forecasting market trends), environmental science (e.g., flood prediction and urban environmental analysis), and medicine and population health (e.g., study of drug implications and disease outbreak)

3: Event mining can be utilized to extract knowledge from a complex system where events can denote any of the system s activities

4: Anything can be defined as a system, from a single component in a jet engine manufacturing line to ubiquitous computing

5: As the system operates, events can be extracted and subsequently mined through a variety of methods

6: Unwanted events can be filtered, anomaly events can be detected, complex event processing (CEP) can be applied to aggregate and monitor the occurrence of correspondence between multiple events, significant patterns of multiple events can be extracted, and so on

7: To understand complex systems and deal with complex patterns, logging systems events with only an ID, name, and timestamp is not enough

8: As mentioned in Chapter 2, events need to be stored as a complex object with all their properties and relationships rather than a relational tuple

9: Event mining algorithms not only need to support symbolic representation of events but also complex relationships such as causality, where one event is the root cause of another.

10: In this chapter, we discuss how event mining can be applied in different domains and overview general requirements and high-level workflow in each domain.

11: 5.1Healthcare and Medicine

12: Through the application of knowledge extraction tools and techniques there is a wealth of data available within the healthcare industry that could provide valuable insights

13: Many providers use electronic medical records (EMRs) to store large quantities of patients data related to test results, medications, prior diagnoses, and other medical history

14: Also, with the advent of wearable sensors and the widespread use of smartphone apps, a copious amount of longitudinal data can be collected from groups of patients, thereby permitting examination and analysis of patterns and trends over time

15: The variety of healthcare data including EMRs, genetic data, biomedical data, and data for public health impose challenges not only on event mining techniques but also on understanding the relationships between these data points that are spread thinly across several dimensions

16: Analyzing relations between temporal events in EMR data has many applications, from risk factor analysis (e.g., risk of readmission or risk of hospital infections) to understanding complex relationships between different diseases and the causes for their comorbidities

17: The primary information embedded in an EMR is referred to as the International Classification of Diseases and Related Health Problems (ICD) and Current Procedural Terminology (CPT) codes

18: ICD-9 and ICD-10 (versions 9 and 10, respectively) are popular coding systems used by healthcare organizations

19: They are primarily intended to code symptoms, diseases, procedures, and other conditions

20: CPT is used to categorize the medical procedures performed on a patient

21: The purpose of these coding systems is to provide a uniform language that accurately describes medical, surgical, diagnostic, and treatment services provided by physicians and other healthcare professionals

22: Despite the abundance of temporal data and events that are encapsulated in EMRs, research on temporal data mining and event mining in medical records is scarce

23: Regardless of the nature of the medical data, many of the typical problems in this domain can be classified into two main categories:

24: Exploratory techniques

25: Predictive techniques

26: Next we will explain each category in more detail.

27: 5.1.1Exploratory Techniques

28: In this approach, the focus is on finding pre-existing medical patterns in the data with no apriori knowledge of what those patterns might be

29: Visual analytics techniques are especially valuable during the discovery process due to the richness of medical data and the depth of domain knowledge that might be needed.

30: Boytcheva et al

31: [2019] designed a system with the aim of examining the comorbidity of diseases and the effect of different treatments on them

32: For example, how the treatment of one disease can affect other co-existing conditions

33: In order to solve this problem, the authors defined three main tasks: (1) Frequent pattern mining and association rules for chronic diseases and related treatments

34: (2) Frequent sequence mining to analyze complex sequences of events with different time spans between them

35: (3) Periodic event mining for finding periodical sequences of certain diseases

36: Figure 5.1High-level architecture of a pattern mining system for understanding comorbidities of multiple chronic diseases

37: Reproduced from Boytcheva et al

38: [2019].

39: Although the authors did not provide any specific algorithm for each task, they applied their system to find associations among schizophrenia, diabetes mellitus type 2, and hyperprolactinemia

40: Figure 5.1 shows their proposed high-level architecture.

41: Many researchers have developed algorithms for exploring temporal events in the healthcare domain with a specific focus on visualization

42: Early works focused mostly on visualizing an individual s medical records, for example, LifeLines [Plaisant et al

43: 1998, Aigner and Miksch 2006], or an individual s care plan [Gotz and Wongsuphasawat 2012]

44: Such tools typically organize data hierarchically to summarize the complex set of values associated with an individual patient

45: More recently, attention has shifted to visualizations of information related to a group of patients

46: This includes a range of tools for visualizing, querying, and sorting through groups of patients event data [Wongsuphasawat and Gotz 2012, Wongsuphasawat and Shneiderman 2009].

47: Wongsuphasawat and Gotz [2011] introduced Outflow, a visualization framework summarizing temporal event data extracted from multiple patient medical records to show aggregate disease evolution statistics for a group of patients

48: Outflow aggregates event sequences into an Outflow graph which is analogous to a state diagram or state transition graph

49: The states are the unique combinations of symptoms that were observed in the data and the edges capture symptom transitions

50: User interaction capabilities such as panning and zooming, symptom selection, filtering, and brushing are incorporated into the framework

51: Although the visualization aspects of the framework are interesting, it lacks pattern mining techniques and user interaction is limited to relatively basic capabilities

52: Futhermore, when the number of event types exceeds a certain threshold, it can lead to an extremely complex web of event pathways.

53: Fails et al

54: [2006] created an interface, called PatternFinder, for visual query and result visualization for searching temporal patterns in multivariate and categorical clinical datasets

55: A dashboard user interface can be used to select an arbitrary number of events and timelags between events to create a pattern

56: The pattern is then translated into an SQL query and the results are shown as a timeline for each patient

57: Events are considered as instantaneous points where day level is the lowest time granularity

58: Each event has a type and values

59: These values can be numeric, such as for a systolic blood pressure reading, or categorical, such as for normal/abnormal blood sugar

60: Although this interface provides a comprehensive dashboard in terms of pattern formulation and query, it is restricted to sequential patterns and it lacks pattern discovery techniques to help analysts bring hidden patterns to the surface

61: Also, the framework lacks data fusion and data transformation modules.

62: Gotz et al

63: [2014] presented a methodology for interactive mining and visual analysis of clinical event patterns using electronic health record data

64: They applied a conventional pattern mining technique, Sequential PAttern Miner (SPAM) [Ayres et al

65: 2002], for pattern discovery and display an ad-hoc visualization of discovered patterns

66: The framework addressed three issues: visual query capabilities to interactively specify pattern definitions, pattern mining techniques to discover important patterns, and interactive visualization techniques which help uncover events that most impact the outcome

67: One major problem with the framework is that events are point events and there is no notion of temporal constraint between events.

68: Pastrello et al

69: [2014] noted the importance and the challenges involved in integrating heterogeneous data from multiple experiments

70: They focused on network analysis as a key technique to integrate, visualize, and extrapolate relevant information from diverse data

71: The authors considered networks as nodes and edges, where individual entities (e.g., genes, proteins, drugs) are represented by nodes and relationships between components (e.g., drug-targeting) are represented by edges

72: They mentioned that network visualization and analysis can be utilized to integrate diverse data, which results in an insightful representation of the system being studied

73: They integrated different datasets from the literature in one network to obtain insight into the gastric cancer problem

74: K lling et al

75: [2012] introduced a web-based tool for visual data mining colocation patterns in multivariate bioimages

76: The system is called Web-based Hyperbolic Image Data Explorer (WHIDE)

77: The authors emphasized that bioimaging techniques rapidly develop toward higher resolution and higher dimension

78: The analysis of such multivariate bio-images requires new techniques to support end users in the analysis of both aspects of such images: space and molecular colocation or interaction

79: Their approach combines principles from machine learning, dimension reduction, and visualization

80: Bowman et al

81: [2012] introduced a neuroimaging visualization framework, called INVIZIAN, for the graphical rendering and the dynamic interaction with the contents of large-scale neuroimaging data sets

82: Their system graphically displays brain surfaces as points in a coordinate space

83: The user can interact with the elements in this space, search over meta-data features, select one or more brain surfaces, and create groups to generate new hypotheses that are worth new experimentation

84: This process enables the user to rapidly explore large collections of neuroimaging data for identifying interesting trends across features and attributes.

85: Patnaik et al

86: [2011, 2012] introduced a system called EMRView, which is one of the first attempts for mining patients history at the big data scale by processing millions of patient records

87: EMRView mines the precedence relationships between events to identify and visualize partially ordered information encoded in key classes of patients

88: The high-level workflow of EMRView is shown in Figure 5.2, and it involves discovering frequent episodes using itemset mining techniques, tracking the order of the diagnostic codes in the discovered episodes, and learning partial orders using PQtree algorithm

89: 5.1.2Predictive Techniques

90: The predictive techniques of classification and regression can help forecast some types of outcome

91: In healthcare, just like other industries, predictors are only useful when the insights they provide can be translated into action

92: This requires a willingness from the healthcare provider s side to intervene at the right time given the prediction results

93: In this section, we explain some use cases of predictions in the healthcare domain

94: Hospital readmissions is an area where hospitals have great incentive to use predictive analytics to improve patient outcome and avoid penalties

95: Generally speaking, a hospital readmission occurs when a patient is admitted to a hospital within a specified time period after being discharged from an earlier (initial) hospitalization

96: Medicare under the Hospital Readmission Reduction Program (HRRP) financially penalizes hospitals with relatively high rates of Medicare readmissions [Boccuti and Casillas 2017]

97: Under the HRRP, hospitals with readmission rates that exceed the national average are penalized through a reduction in payments across all of their Medicare admissions, not just those which resulted in readmissions

98: Highlighting the rapidly growing interest in this problem, some recent papers focused on predicting readmission from EMR data

99: In 2015, the number of papers on hospital readmissions prediction approximately doubled compared to the previous year (Figure 5.3)

100: The motivation behind the research efforts in using predictive analytics in healthcare is improving outcomes while reducing costs and avoiding reimbursement penalties for hospitals

101: Figure 5.2Workflow of EMRView system

102: The three tasks of (i) mining parallel episodes, (ii) tracking serial extensions, and (iii) learning partial orders are performed to uncover partially ordered information in EMR data

103: Reproduced from Patnaik et al

104: [2011].

105: Figure 5.3Opportunities for improving patient care while reducing costs and avoiding penalties have resulted in rapidly growing interest in predicting hospital readmissions.

106: Ben-Chetrit et al

107: [2012] performed a retrospective observational study on physician discharge notes and used univariate and multivariate logistic regression analyses to find factors that were significantly associated with readmission (P<0.05).

108: Using machine learning techniques, Shameer et al

109: [2017] designed a multi-step modeling strategy, using the naive Bayes algorithm, for readmissions prediction in heart failure patients

110: First, they created individual models for Boolean classification of readmitted versus non-readmitted

111: Then, features contributing to predictive risk from the model in the previous stage were combined into a composite model using a correlation-based feature selection (CFS) method

112: With this approach, authors could reach an area under the curve (AUC) of 78% and accuracy of 83.19%

113: Some other studies utilized artificial neural networks [Jamei et al

114: 2017] or decision trees [Shams et al

115: 2015] for this problem and concluded that factors such as longer length of stay, disease severity index, being discharged from a hospital, and primary language other than English were associated with increased risks of being readmitted within 30 days.

116: Predictive techniques have been used in other domains such as mortality prediction [Rose 2013, Wu et al

117: 2010], intensive care unit admission [Desautels et al

118: 2017, Wengerter et al

119: 2018], predicting if a particular treatment is beneficial for a specific patient, and disease prognoses prediction [Cruz and Wishart 2006].

120: Aside from research in academia, the common challenge in healthcare applications is: How can hospitals successfully apply promising ideas from academic research to a fully developed system in a hospital information technology (IT) environment, given the inherent problems of predictive models (e.g., the need for retraining) and data privacy issues

121: 5.2Biological Data Analysis

122: In recent years, rapid developments in genomics and proteomics have generated a large amount of biological data

123: Genomics can be broadly defined as the systematic study of genes, their functions, and their interactions

124: Proteomics is the study of proteins, protein complexes, and their interactions

125: With the advent of high-throughput technologies in biology and biotechnology, we are witnessing a paradigm shift from traditional hypothesis-driven to data-driven analysis for understanding large amounts of biological data

126: One major challenge, however, is managing the variety and complexity of data types, the hierarchy, and the need to acquire data that spans many modalities

127: Each data type requires a unique analytical approach for understanding and inferring structure or patterns from them

128: Sequence data that is associated with the DNA of various species have grown enormously with the development of automated sequencing technology

129: DNA is a long, chainlike molecule which has two strands twisted into a double helix

130: The two strands are made up of simpler molecules called nucleotides

131: Each nucleotide is composed of one of the four possible letters: A, C, G, and T

132: DNA sequencing is the process of determining the nucleotide sequence of a given DNA fragment

133: DNA sequencing is being used to refine the human genome sequence and to provide information regarding normal and disease-associated variations in the human genome

134: Two other central molecular building blocks are RNA and proteins

135: RNA molecules are very similar to DNA and are also composed of four nucleotides (A, C, G, and U)

136: Proteins are chains of 20 different basic units called amino acids

137: In computational biology, data presents itself in the form of DNA, RNA, and protein sequences

138: This gives rise to an opportunity to employ unsupervised event mining techniques to discover recurrent patterns in these sequences

139: Generally in event mining, given two sequences of equal length we can define a measure of similarity by considering distances between corresponding elements of the two sequences

140: When the sequences consist of symbolic data, for example, in DNA sequences, we need to define the similarity between every pair of symbols

141: When sequences are of different lengths, it is not possible to accumulate distances between corresponding elements of the sequences

142: This motivates an important subproblem of sequence matching, which is sequence alignment

143: Dynamic time warping and its variations are a well-known technique for finding an optimal alignment between two temporal sequences, and it has been used for sequence classification and matching [M ller 2007, Skutkova et al

144: 2013, Hou et al

145: 2017]

146: The detailed analysis of DNA or protein sequences is beyond the scope of this chapter

147: Interested readers are encouraged to refer to Ewens and Grant [2006] and Cohen [2004] for more details.

148: In addition to sequence matching and sequence alignment in biological sequences, motif discovery has interesting biological applications

149: A sequence motif is a nucleotide or amino acid sequence pattern that is widespread and has been proven or assumed to have biological significance

150: In general, motifs could represent patterns in any kind of biological sequences such as DNA sequences, RNA sequences, and protein sequences

151: In DNA, a motif may correspond to a protein binding site

152: In proteins, a motif may correspond to the active site of an enzyme or a structural unit necessary for proper folding of the protein

153: Motifs can be represented as regular expressions, hidden Markov models, or stochastic context-free grammars (for RNA or DNA sequences)

154: For example, a motif in a protein can be: N, followed by anything except P, followed by either S or T, followed by anything except P

155: This motif can be written as NP[ST]P, where X means any amino acid except X, and [XYZ] means either X or Y or Z.

156: In bioinformatics, motif discovery is becoming very important because they represent conserved sequences that can be biologically meaningful

157: Many algorithms have been developed for discovering motifs and searching databases to find matches to a given motif

158: Some of these algorithms include anchored gapless local alignment of multiple sequences (A-GLAM) [Kim et al

159: 2006], BioProspector [Liu et al

160: 2000], MDscan [Liu et al

161: 2002], and MEME [Bailey and Elkan 1994]

162: Most motif discovery algorithms are limited to gapless motifs

163: The main reason is that by allowing gaps the possible number of potential variations increase exponentially

164: Regular expressions have been used to find motifs with gaps

165: However, they have trouble capturing subtle motifs because they specify exactly what residues and spacers are allowed at each position, and do not allow a better match in one part to compensate for a worse match in another part

166: Since they make very detailed specifications, it may also be hard to discover accurate regular expressions from small numbers of examples

167: Gapped local alignment of motifs (GLAM2) is another algorithm proposed for discovering motifs that allows insertion or deletion of bases in a genome

168: A companion method called GLAM2SCAN searches sequence databases using such motifs [Frith et al

169: 2008]

170: A motif in GLAM2 has a certain number, W, of key positions

171: The idea is that the key positions hold amino acids or nucleotides that are important for the motif s function

172: An instance of the motif is a string of residues (amino acids or nucleotides), where each residue either occupies one of the key positions or is inserted between key positions

173: GLAM2 searches for an alignment of substrings of the input sequences to a series of key positions

174: The number of key positions is optimized by the algorithm

175: In order to define the strongest motif, GLAM2 uses a formula to assign a numeric score to any given alignment

176: Then, the task is to find the alignment with the maximum score

177: GLAM2 utilizes a simple statistical model of a motif, with position-specific residue probabilities, position-specific deletion probabilities, and position-specific insertion probabilities

178: If each key position has characteristic probabilities, i, containing the ith residue type, is the probability of each key position being deleted and x(1 ) is the probability that x residues are inserted between two key positions, This model can be regarded as a hidden Markov model, where i are emission probabilities and and are transition probabilities

179: Figure 5.4 shows motif alignment for multiple segments of sequences, where key positions are denoted by the * symbol.

180: To analyze the functionality of the algorithm, the authors applied GLAM2 to discover a model of a bipartite DNA-binding motif associated with the LMO2 gene

181: Figure 5.5 shows two motifs, a CAGGTG and a GATA, separated by a spacer of length 8 to 10 basepairs determined using the CAST-ing technique [Wadman et al

182: 1997]

183: CASTing is a procedure for identification of consensus sequences of DNA to which a protein, for example, a transcription factor, may bind

184: In Figure 5.6, when 31 random protein complexes are given as input, GLAM2 discovers the CAGGTG motif and extends the GATA motif on the right by three columns

192: 5.3Predictive Maintenance

193: Manufacturing and industrial operations can obtain great benefits by analyzing event-driven information, sensor data streams, and machine/maintenance logs to provide proactive recommendations

194: Manufacturing failures cause significant problems in the reliability of industrial processes and associated costs with downtime of equipment

195: Thus, there is an increasing demand for reducing unexpected failures, eliminating unscheduled downtimes, and minimizing maintenance-related costs

196: Proactive decisions regarding the replacement of a part in predictive maintenance requires a balance between the cost due to premature replacement of the part and the cost of unexpected failure

197: Hence, remaining useful life (RUL) estimation using real-time data (e.g., through sensors and event logs) is an emerging area of research [Si et al

198: 2011]

199: Moreover, the use of sensors that gather huge amounts of data from industrial business processes leads to the evolution of Internet of Things (IoTs), which in turn enables the identification and prediction of deviations in the production process in comparison to the scheduled performance and the recommendation of the appropriate actions at the appropriate time [Feldman et al

200: 2013]

201: Doing so requires the development of event-driven data processing systems that are able to handle very high frequency events from various sources, and coupling prognostic-based decision methods with sensor-based, event-driven architectures that can support efficient processing of events and improve scalability

240: 5.4Business Intelligence

241: Forrester Research defined business intelligence (BI) as a set of methodologies, processes, architectures, and technologies that transform raw data into meaningful and useful information used to enable more effective strategic, tactical, and operational insights and decision-making [Evelson and Norman 2008]

242: BI has many dimensions including predictive analytics, data mining, text mining, process mining, CEP, business performance management, and benchmarking

243: The aim of BI is to identify new opportunities and to implement an effective strategy based on insights that provide businesses with a competitive market advantage and long-term stability [Rud 2009].

244: Leat [2007] defined the evolutionary steps in adoption of BI analytics in five levels, with the evolution of event-based analysis appearing in the higher levels of the hierarchy:

245: Level 1, awareness: This level shows businesses are aware of the importance of BI in their decision-making but there are few BI capabilities

246: BI is primarily based on batched reports with some online analytical processing (OLAP)

247: Level 2, development: There is basic and non-integrated BI capabilities in place

248: Still, BI is mostly based on batched reports with some OLAP and predictive modeling and data mining

249: Level 3, practice: This level implements basic BI capabilities

250: Analytical and predictive modeling and mining grows.

251: Level 4, optimization: This level integrates BI practices into daily operations

252: BI is mostly based on data mining and adapts event-based analysis to some extent.

253: Level 5, leadership: In this level, the organization is led based on predictive models deployed in an event-triggered environment.

254: Recently, the application of CEP in BI has gained some attention

255: Generally, BI platforms provide companies with the ability to model, manage, and predict business processes

256: CEP is a technology for tracking and analyzing streams of data from multiple sources to infer patterns that suggest complicated situations

257: When CEP is applied in the context of BI, events coming from different event sources can trigger a business process or influence the execution of the process

258: Moreover, complex events (e.g., the relation between multiple business level events) can also be relevant for the execution of other business processes

259: One important aspect of CEP in BI is the use of technology to proactively define and analyze the most critical opportunities and risks in an enterprise.

260: Ammon et al

261: [2008] proposed a high-level reference architecture for using CEP in business process management

262: The architecture has two separate sets of components

263: Business processes are demonstrated with workflow components

264: The main elements of an event processing system are a different set of components that accompany workflow components

265: The role of business analysts or workflow modelers is to identify, analyze, and optimize business processes, while the role of an event modeler is to define which business level events need to be monitored in a dashboard, which alerts should be sent to which roles in the organization, and which actions should be started automatically if a certain event pattern occurs

266: 5.5Computer Networks

267: Network infrastructure is the hardware and software resources of an entire network that enable network connectivity, communication, operations, and management of an enterprise network

268: A typical network infrastructure includes hardware (e.g., wireless routers, switches, and LAN cards), software (e.g., network operations and management, firewall, and network security applications), and services (e.g., DSL, wireless protocols, and IP addressing)

269: The network infrastructure of companies like Amazon is even more complex since they have several international data centers

270: These large-scale complex networks introduce many difficulties in design, operation, and maintenance

271: Equipment failure, communication error, and system misconfiguration are typical problems that need to be addressed by system administrators

272: Additionally, complex network infrastructure are heterogeneous, consisting of components made by various manufacturers

273: Also, software components running on the network often generate a huge number of messages and alerts that cannot be processed and addressed manually

274: Different network elements in large complex networks collect alerts and messages in log files, indicating the equipment status in real time

275: Mostly, the contents of these log files are unstructured and system events are stored as short messages in plain text

276: The log files therefore need to be analyzed and appropriate events need to be extracted from the logs

277: Converting log messages to events provides the capability of canonically describing the semantics of log data and improves the ability of correlating and root cause analysis across multiple log components

278: The task of event recognition from log files is beyond the scope of this chapter

279: There are different classification [Androulidakis et al

280: 2009, Kruegel et al

281: 2003], clustering [Tang and Li 2010, Tang et al

282: 2011, Makanju et al

283: 2009], and parsing techniques that can be applied to extract events from logs.

284: Once events are extracted from log files, different event mining and pattern discovery techniques (see Chapter 3 for in depth explanation) can be utilized for automatic root cause analysis of failures in the networks

285: Lou et al

286: [2010] proposed a framework to find intercomponent dependencies from unstructured logs

287: First, log messages are parsed to find log key pairs and some parameters

288: Then, dependent log key pairs belonging to different components are found by co-occurrence analysis and parameter correspondence

289: To estimate the dependency direction of each dependent log key pair, Bayesian decision theory is utilized at the end

290: Khan et al

291: [2008] presented a tool called Dustminer that finds complex and unexpected interactions between multiple, often individually non-faulty, components in a sensor network that lead to a highly degraded mode of operation

292: Another body of research uses temporal pattern mining from sequential log data for root cause analysis

293: Unlike traditional methods that use predefined time windows, these techniques mine temporal dependencies between events to find fluctuating and interleaved time lags

294: Yan et al

295: [2012] described a root cause analysis platform for service quality management in large IP networks

296: The platform collects data from various logging and performance monitoring systems at different network layers in the ISP, and includes a library of event definitions, network topology, service dependencies, and dependency relationship rules

297: For root cause analysis, the platform utilizes spatiotemporal correlation, rule-based reasoning, and Bayesian inference
