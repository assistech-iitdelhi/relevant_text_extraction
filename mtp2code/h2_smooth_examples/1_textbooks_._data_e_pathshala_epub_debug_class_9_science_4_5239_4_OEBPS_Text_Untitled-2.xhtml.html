42: 4.2 The Structure of an Atom

48: 4.2.1 Thomson s model of an atom

49: Thomson proposed the model of an atom to be similar to that of a Christmas pudding

50: The electrons, in a sphere of positive charge, were like currants (dry fruits) in a spherical Christmas pudding

51: We can also think of a watermelon, the positive charge in the atom is spread all over like the red edible part of the watermelon, while the electrons are studded in the positively charged sphere, like the seeds in the watermelon (Figure 4.1).

53: Thomson proposed that:

54: (i) An atom consists of a positively charged sphere and the electrons are embedded in it.

55: (ii) The negative and positive charges are equal in magnitude

56: So, the atom as a whole is electrically neutral.

57: Although Thomson s model explained that atoms are electrically neutral, the results of experiments carried out by other scientists could not be explained by this model, as we will see below.

99: Drawbacks of Rutherford s model of the atom

152: 4.4 Valency

153: We have learnt how the electrons in an atom are arranged in different shells/orbits

154: The electrons present in the outermost shell of an atom are known as the valence electrons.

155: From the Bohr-Bury scheme, we also know that the outermost shell of an atom can accommodate a maximum of 8 electrons

156: It was observed that the atoms of elements, completely filled with 8 electrons in the outermost shell show little chemical activity

157: In other words, their combining capacity or valency is zero

158: Of these inert elements, the helium atom has two electrons in its outermost shell and all other elements have atoms with eight electrons in the outermost shell.

160: The combining capacity of the atoms of elements, that is, their tendency to react and form molecules with atoms of the same or different elements, was thus explained as an attempt to attain a fully-filled outermost shell

161: An outermost-shell, which had eight electrons was said to possess an octet

162: Atoms would thus react, so as to achieve an octet in the outermost shell

163: This was done by sharing, gaining or losing electrons

164: The number of electrons gained, lost or shared so as to make the octet of electrons in the outermost shell, gives us directly the combining capacity of the element, that is, the valency discussed in the previous chapter

165: For example, hydrogen/lithium/sodium atoms contain one electron each in their outermost shell, therefore each one of them can lose one electron

166: So, they are said to have valency of one

167: Can you tell, what is valency of magnesium and aluminium

170: For example, the fluorine atom has 7 electrons in the outermost shell, and its valency could be 7

171: But it is easier for fluorine to gain one electron instead of losing seven electrons

172: Hence, its valency is determined by subtracting seven electrons from the octet and this gives you a valency of one for fluorine

205: 4.6 Isotopes

206: In nature, a number of atoms of some elements have been identified, which have the same atomic number but different mass numbers

207: For example, take the case of hydrogen atom, it has three atomic species, namely protium (11H), deuterium (21H or D) and tritium (13H or T)

208: The atomic number of each one is 1, but the mass number is 1, 2 and 3, respectively

209: Other such examples are (i) carbon, 126C and 146C, (ii) chlorine, 3517Cl and 3717Cl, etc.

210: On the basis of these examples, isotopes are defined as the atoms of the same element, having the same atomic number but different mass numbers

211: Therefore, we can say that there are three isotopes of hydrogen atom, namely protium, deuterium and tritium.

212: Many elements consist of a mixture of isotopes

213: Each isotope of an element is a pure substance

214: The chemical properties of isotopes are similar but their physical properties are different.

215: Chlorine occurs in nature in two isotopic forms, with masses 35 u and 37 u in the ratio of 3:1

216: Obviously, the question arises: what should we take as the mass of chlorine atom

217: Let us find out.

218: The average atomic mass of chlorine atom, on the basis of above data, will be

219: The mass of an atom of any natural element is taken as the average mass of all the naturally occuring atoms of that element

220: If an element has no isotopes, then the mass of its atom would be the same as the sum of protons and neutrons in it

221: But if an element occurs in isotopic forms, then we have to know the percentage of each isotopic form and then the average mass is calculated.

222: This does not mean that any one atom of chlorine has a fractional mass of 35.5 u

223: It means that if you take a certain amount of chlorine, it will contain both isotopes of chlorine and the average mass is 35.5 u.
